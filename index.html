Building support quality measurement at scale

Measurement systems & service evaluation · Customer Support · 2022–2025
Problem & Framing

How do we measure support quality in a way that is reliable, sensitive enough to detect real differences, developmental for agents, and reflective of the actual user experience, without runaway cost? In 2022, I led the validation of the existing quality framework and found that its scores did not predict user experience or support outcomes. This raised a fundamental question: were we measuring quality accurately at all? Answering that question became a multi-year programme to redesign how support quality was measured.

Diagnosis & Methods

I began by observing agents, reviewing support transcripts, and defining measurement requirements through both academic literature and leadership priorities. Throughout the programme, I ran recurring interviews, shadowing sessions, and asynchronous feedback loops with agents and auditors. This ensured the framework reflected both agent behaviour (including motivations and gaming risks) and auditors' ability to score consistently and accurately. To evaluate the existing system, I ran randomised controlled pilots against a third-party tool and applied factor analysis, inter-item correlation analysis, and regression modelling to assess construct validity and predictive validity against user and business outcomes.

Findings

The third-party tool was not predictive of either the quality framework or user outcomes. Its automated scoring performed no better than noise, while increasing burden on manual auditors. The analysis also revealed weaknesses within the framework itself. Factor analysis exposed redundant items that added complexity without improving measurement quality.

Iteration & Influence

I partnered with Data Science to independently validate the findings and presented the evidence to leadership through three rounds of review and refinement. The result was a reversal of an earlier decision to purchase the third-party solution in favour of building an internal system. I then led the redesign of the framework: reducing the scorecard by roughly half while maintaining sensitivity, introducing a nested near-real-time sampling strategy, moving to agent-level scoring, and designing an audit-of-auditors layer to measure reviewer quality.

Impact

The framework I authored and validated became the foundation of the internal quality platform and its AI automation layer. Today it supports day-to-day quality assurance globally across all supported languages and thousands of agents operating through multiple vendor partners.

Diagnosing and improving support resolution

Behavioural diagnosis & service improvement · Customer Support · 2021–2024
Problem & Framing

Customer satisfaction was being negatively impacted by unresolved support issues, yet the organisation lacked a clear understanding of why some cases were not reaching resolution. A commonly held belief was that agents were under excessive time pressure and therefore unable to fully resolve issues before closing cases. Before proposing solutions, I wanted to understand whether this explanation was actually true and identify the behaviours and operational constraints driving poor user outcomes.

Diagnosis & Methods

I began by introducing a recurring agent survey to establish an ongoing signal of friction points within the support experience and identify areas requiring deeper investigation. Survey findings highlighted resolution challenges, which I followed up through a focused mixed-method programme. This combined transcript reviews, screen recordings with timestamp analysis, existing quality data, structured reviews of reopened cases, on-site shadowing, and focus groups with agents. Because support operations were delivered through third-party vendors, I also partnered with vendor organisations to understand how their performance targets and incentives shaped agent behaviour.

Findings

The evidence contradicted the prevailing assumption that time pressure was the primary issue. Agents generally had time available within their workflows. Instead, the research revealed that slow escalation processes were preventing timely resolution. To meet case-closure targets set by vendors, agents often closed cases before escalations were complete, leaving users without a fully resolved outcome. The work also identified supporting process failures, including limited visibility into escalation progress, difficulty transferring misrouted cases, and insufficient mechanisms for managing secondary issues raised during support interactions. Taken together, the findings showed that customer dissatisfaction stemmed from process and incentive design rather than workload constraints.

Iteration & Intervention

I translated the diagnosis into a set of targeted interventions, including increased visibility into escalation status, clearer expectations for escalation turnaround times, and deliberate friction to discourage premature closure of unresolved cases.

Impact

Using regression analysis to control for existing trends and operational differences, I evaluated the resulting changes and found that the closure-friction intervention delivered the largest improvement in customer satisfaction observed that year. Beyond the immediate uplift, the agent survey and quality framework checks became enduring mechanisms for identifying emerging resolution issues earlier, reducing a longstanding organisational blind spot and improving the team's ability to iterate on tooling and process.

Creating a behavioural growth playbook

Behavioural strategy & experimentation · Consumer Hardware · 2018–2021
Problem & Framing

A major hardware business was launching new products across both owned and third-party retail channels. While discounting could increase sales, it came at a direct cost to the business. I saw an opportunity to test whether behavioural interventions could provide a more scalable and sustainable way to improve conversion and sales performance while building internal capability for future launches.

Diagnosis & Methods

I prioritised and led a portfolio of behavioural research initiatives focused on key moments in the customer journey. In-app promotions: I designed behaviourally informed multi-arm randomised controlled trials to optimise promotional messaging and offers that drove device sales globally. I owned experiment design and behavioural strategy, partnering with Data Science on analysis. Product-service bundling: Working with a colleague, I used conjoint analysis to identify the combinations of products and services that created the strongest value proposition for customers. Retail agent incentives: I partnered with third-party retailers to run field experiments testing how different incentive structures influenced sales behaviour among retail staff. Alongside the research itself, I worked with product, marketing, and commercial stakeholders to translate behavioural science principles into practical guidance that could be reused beyond the individual studies.

Findings

Across all three programmes, the research identified statistically significant interventions that improved commercial outcomes and informed bundling, incentive, and marketing strategy. More broadly, the work demonstrated that behavioural interventions could meaningfully influence purchasing behaviour in this context without relying solely on discounting or additional spend.

Iteration & Scaling

To ensure the approach could be applied beyond these individual projects, I created a behavioural science playbook and delivered training sessions across teams, translating research findings into repeatable principles and practical tools for future launches. This shifted the work from a set of isolated experiments to a reusable approach for optimising customer decision-making across the business.

Impact

The findings informed launch strategy, product bundling, retail incentives, and promotional activity, generating evidence with multi-million-pound impact when scaled. More importantly, the work established and validated a lower-cost behavioural approach to sales optimisation that teams could continue applying long after the original studies concluded.

Understanding hidden barriers to benefit uptake

Behavioural measurement & public-interest research · Civic Technology · 2020–2021
Problem & Framing

During the early stages of COVID-19, searches related to government benefits tripled, yet many eligible individuals were not accessing available support. One potential explanation was that benefit-seeking carried an implicit association with shame. Because people are often unwilling or unable to report these attitudes directly, traditional survey methods alone were unlikely to capture the true barrier.

Diagnosis & Methods

I designed a three-phase mixed-method study. First, I used open card-sorting exercises and Reddit text analysis to understand how people conceptualised benefit-seeking. Next, I conducted a quantitative study combining self-report measures, demographic data, and an Implicit Association Test (IAT) designed to measure associations between benefit-seeking and shame versus pride, as well as social versus transactional framing. To ensure methodological robustness, I first validated the IAT approach using a flowers-versus-insects proof of concept (d = 0.77) before deploying it within a nationally representative US sample of more than 300 participants. The resulting data was analysed using regression, ANOVA, and MANOVA techniques.

Findings

The study identified a meaningful implicit association between benefit-seeking and shame that was largely missed by self-report measures. The effect was strongest for food assistance programmes and significantly improved prediction of both willingness to apply for benefits and previous benefit-seeking behaviour.

Impact

The research provided evidence for a behavioural barrier that had previously been difficult to measure and gave the team a clearer understanding of the mechanisms influencing uptake. The findings informed how the relevant design approach could be applied to smart cards. The solution was implemented after I had moved on, so I cannot speak to the eventual product impact. The project was delivered on an approximately £1,000 proof-of-concept budget, without sharing user-level data, and within the constraints of influence available through the Search results page.
